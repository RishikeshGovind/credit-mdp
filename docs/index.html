The findings What surprised us
A small study, built on real loan data

When a bank sets your loan, it changes how risky you are.

Say a bank is deciding whether to give you a loan, and what interest rate to charge. A lower rate is easier to pay back, so you are less likely to miss payments. A higher rate earns the bank more money each month, but it strains your budget, so you are more likely to fall behind.

So here is the catch. The rate the bank picks does not just measure your risk. It changes it. Most banks ignore this. They guess how risky you are first, as if the rate had nothing to do with it, and only then pick the rate. This little project asks what happens if you take that loop seriously.

Chapter 1

The usual way, and the thing it misses

what banks usually do

Guess first, then decide

A bank looks at your history and guesses how likely you are to miss payments. Then it sets a cutoff. If your guessed risk is low enough, you get the loan at the going rate. If not, you are turned down.

This is simple and easy to check. It also works pretty well, which is part of the story. But it has a blind spot.

the blind spot

The rate is part of the risk

The guess is made as if the rate were already fixed. But a cheaper loan is a safer loan, because it is easier to pay back. The bank's own choice feeds back into the very risk it was trying to measure.

So this is not really a guessing problem with a decision bolted on the end. The decision and the risk are tangled together.

Chapter 2

Three things make this harder than it looks

A real bank is not handing out one loan. It is making thousands of these calls, over months, with limited money, and trying to hit several goals at once. Three things pull against each other.

1

The rate moves the risk

Charge more and you earn more per loan, but you also push some people into missing payments. The price and the risk are linked.

2

The money runs out

Banks have to set money aside for safety, and there is only so much of it. Lend too fast early on and you have nothing left when better borrowers show up later. Losses eat into it too.

3

There is no single goal

The bank wants profit, but also steady losses, a safe cushion, and fair treatment of different groups. You cannot max out all four at once. Something always gives.

Chapter 3 · what we found

First, a risk model that reacts to the price

Before anything else we needed a way to guess risk that actually responds to the rate. We built one on a thousand real loan records, then checked two things. Does it match what really happened, and does a higher rate really raise the predicted risk?

Left: when the model says a group is risky, that group really did miss payments more often, so the model is trustworthy. Right: raising the rate lifts the predicted risk. The flat grey line is the old way, which pretends the rate does not matter.
What we found

So should the bank charge less? Mostly not.

This was the big question, and the honest answer surprised us. If charging less keeps people from defaulting, maybe the bank should drop its rates. But a loan earns interest for years, while a default is a one time loss. The years of interest usually win. So the best rate barely moves.

Left: the best rate to charge stays near the top for most borrowers, even when the rate strongly affects risk. It only drops for the shakiest borrowers. Right: for those shaky borrowers, the most profitable rate slides lower as the rate matters more.
💡

The honest takeaway

At the level the real data points to, charging less to lower risk does not pay off on price. A plain bank that just charges the going rate is close to right. The interesting payoff shows up somewhere else, in who gets a loan and how the bank balances its goals.

What we found

Every strategy gives something up

We let a computer try thousands of lending strategies and kept the best ones. No single strategy wins on everything. Each one trades profit against risk, against the safety cushion, against fairness. The two usual approaches show up as single dots, and you can see exactly what each one quietly gives away.

The dots toward the top right are the good strategies. The orange star is the usual bank approach. It gets beaten on every goal at once. The purple cross chases profit only, and pays for it with bumpier losses and a bigger safety bill. Lighter colours are fairer.
What we found

Watching it play out over time

Because the bank lends month after month, it helps to watch the strategies run. The grabby ones lend fast, pile up losses, and burn through their safety money. The balanced ones pace themselves and stay in much better shape.

The usual approach (orange) and the profit chaser (purple) rack up the most losses. Our balanced and fair strategies keep losses low and hold onto their safety cushion.
What we found

What does fairness cost?

One of the goals is treating different groups of applicants equally. We can measure how much profit the bank gives up to close that gap. It turns out you can get all the way to equal treatment for a real but limited cost.

Moving left means treating the two groups more equally, and it costs some profit. The usual bank approach (orange star) sits below the line, which means it is unfair and leaving money on the table.
Chapter 4

What surprised us, and where this is weak

A portfolio piece is more useful when it owns its limits, so here they are in plain terms.

  1. 1Charging less did not really help. The years of interest a good loan earns outweigh the odd default, so the best price barely moves. The clever loop matters for who gets a loan, not for the rate.
  2. 2The simple approach often ties the fancy one. If a bank only cares about profit, the plain profit chaser already does great. The extra machinery earns its keep only when you care about more than one goal.
  3. 3We measured a link, not proof. The connection between rate and risk comes from past data, not a controlled test. We treat it as a careful estimate and check what happens if it is stronger or weaker.
  4. 4The data is a stand in. We used a thousand real consumer loans from an old public dataset, plus real Irish averages for scale. It is a fair sketch of the problem, not a live mortgage book. The exact numbers are illustrations.
  5. 5Fairness here is narrow. We only check whether two groups get approved at similar rates. That is a starting point, not the whole of fair lending.
