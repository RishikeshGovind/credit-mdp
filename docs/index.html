A small study, built on real loan data

When a bank sets your loan, it changes how risky you are.

Say a bank is deciding whether to give you a loan, and what interest rate to charge. A lower rate is easier to pay back, so you are less likely to miss payments. A higher rate earns the bank more, but it strains your budget, so you are more likely to fall behind.

So here is the catch. The rate the bank picks does not just measure your risk. It changes it. Most banks ignore this. They guess how risky you are first, as if the rate had nothing to do with it, and only then pick the rate. This project takes that loop seriously and asks whether it helps.

Chapter 1

The usual way, and the thing it misses

what banks usually do

Guess first, then decide

A bank looks at your history and guesses how likely you are to miss payments. Then it sets a cutoff. If your guessed risk is low enough, you get the loan at the going rate. If not, you are turned down.

This is simple and easy to check. It also works pretty well, which is part of the story. But it has a blind spot.

the blind spot

The rate is part of the risk

The guess is made as if the rate were already fixed. But a cheaper loan is a safer loan, because it is easier to pay back. The bank's own choice feeds back into the very risk it was trying to measure.

And here the data backs it up. We use real loans that record both the rate charged and whether the loan defaulted, so the loop is sitting right there in the numbers.

Chapter 2

Three things make this harder than it looks

A real bank is not handing out one loan. It is making thousands of these calls, over months, with limited money, and trying to hit several goals at once. Three things pull against each other.

1

The rate moves the risk

Charge more and you earn more per loan, but you also push some people into missing payments. The price and the risk are linked.

2

The money runs out

Safety money is tied up while a loan is live and frees up only when it finishes. Lend too fast early and you have nothing left when good applicants show up later.

3

There is no single goal

The bank wants profit, but also steady losses, a safe cushion, and fair treatment of different groups. You cannot max out all four at once. Something always gives.

Chapter 3 · what we found

First, a risk model that reacts to the price

We built a risk model on the real loans and checked two things. Does it match what really happened, and does a higher rate really raise the predicted risk? Because the data records the actual rate and the actual outcome, the second part is learned, not assumed.

Left: when the model calls a group risky, that group really did default more often. It is a weak model (about 0.61 AUC), and the chart is honest about that. Right: a higher offered rate raises predicted risk. The flat line is the old way, which pretends the rate does not matter.
What we found

So should the bank charge less? Mostly not.

This was the big question, and the honest answer surprised us. A loan earns interest for years, while a default is a one-off loss, and the years of interest usually win. So the best rate barely moves for typical borrowers. For the riskiest borrowers it does start to drop, right around the level the real data points to.

Left: the best rate to charge stays at the top for most borrowers, and only bends down for the riskiest ones, near the real-data level. Right: for those risky borrowers, the most profitable rate slides lower as the rate matters more.
💡

The honest takeaway

Charging less to lower risk is on the edge of mattering for risky borrowers, but not a dramatic effect overall. A plain bank that just charges the going rate is close to right on price. The real payoff is elsewhere, in who gets a loan and how the bank balances its goals.

What we found

Every strategy gives something up

We let a computer try thousands of lending strategies and kept the best ones. No single strategy wins on everything. Each one trades profit against risk, against the safety cushion, against fairness. The two usual approaches show up as single dots, and you can see exactly what each one quietly gives away.

The dots toward the bottom right are the good strategies. The orange star is the usual bank approach, and it gets beaten on every goal at once. The purple cross chases profit only, and pays with bumpier losses. Lighter colours are fairer.
What we found

Watching it play out over months

Because the bank lends month after month, it helps to watch the strategies run. The grabby ones tie up all their safety money early and pile up losses. The balanced ones pace themselves and stay in much better shape.

The usual approach and the profit chaser rack up the most losses. The balanced and fair strategies keep losses low and hold onto their safety cushion.
What we found

What does fairness cost?

One of the goals is treating different groups of applicants equally. The data has no information on age, sex, or race, so we use income as a stand-in for access, and ask whether lower- and higher-income applicants get approved at similar rates.

Moving left means treating the two income groups more equally, and it costs some profit. The usual bank approach (orange star) sits below the line, which means it is unfair and leaving money on the table.
Chapter 4

What surprised us, and where this is weak

A portfolio piece is more useful when it owns its limits, so here they are in plain terms.

  1. 1Charging less mostly did not move the price. The years of interest a good loan earns outweigh the odd default, so the best rate stays high. It only bends for the riskiest borrowers. The clever loop matters more for who gets a loan than for the rate.
  2. 2The simple approach often ties the fancy one. If a bank only cares about profit, the plain profit chaser already does great. The extra machinery earns its keep only when you care about more than one goal.
  3. 3We measured a link, not proof. The bank set the rate from risk, so the rate-default link is confounded. We control for the obvious risk drivers and stress-test the rest, but it is an association, not a clean cause.
  4. 4No demographic data. We can only check access by income, not by protected characteristics. That is a real limit of this dataset.
  5. 5It is a sampling method, not reinforcement learning. Random search, then a small genetic refinement, with each strategy scored by simulation. We are careful not to oversell it.
